## 完整内容

### Anthropic 作为 AI 安全与研究公司

Daniela Amodei 解释，Anthropic 专注于如何以稳健安全的方式开发工具和系统，贯穿整个技术栈。他们使用名为宪法式 AI 的技术，帮助将道德感灌输给 Claude。多份文档帮助通过宪法式 AI 训练 Claude，包括联合国人权宣言等。在发布新模型或新产品时，他们使用负责任扩展政策（RSP）框架。RSP 本质上是一套指南，说明如何以及何时发布模型以确保安全。他们使用不同的 AI 安全等级，共五个，实际上仿照生物安全等级设计。他们寻找的是定义一套具体的安全和安保标准。

### 平衡研究与商业化

Amodei 表示，经营像 Anthropic 这样的公司最有趣和不寻常的一点是，他们几乎是研究实验室、科技创业公司和公益使命的结合体。思考如何平衡这三者以服务使命并为客户创造价值，是在这样的地方工作的巨大乐趣和兴奋。就研究到产品的平衡而言，他们发现产品与研究之间的协作确实使两个团队都更强大。特别是，随着新研究能力的开发，实际上会开辟产品中以前不存在的新途径。最近的一个例子是 Claude 在编写代码方面特别有用。过去两三个月开发的许多产品都围绕让软件开发者的流程更简单，无论他们是在大企业、中型公司还是初创公司。如果不知道模型在编码方面会很好，他们可能不会那样设计产品。在某种程度上，存在一个迭代过程：随着更多客户使用他们进行编码，他们也在帮助训练模型使其在编码方面更强大，然后在该领域提供更多产品支持。

### 公益公司（PBC）

Amodei 解释，创办 Anthropic 时，他们花了不少时间思考要注册为什么类型的结构。他们有一点优势是近距离见过不同模式。注册为 PBC 时考虑的是，什么是平衡他们预见的商业利益与创办像 Anthropic 这样如此投入安全等主题的公司的公益意图的非常务实的方式。他们最终决定公益公司是这两个领域的很好的融合。它是 C 公司，所以在大多数方面与特拉华州 C 公司的结构非常相似。但一个例外是，他们有能力在商业需求与公益利益之间取得平衡。这对他们来说似乎是他们试图做的事情的非常务实的方法。因此她认为，根据正在创办的创业公司的目标，如果业务的某个组成部分真正旨在平衡公共利益与正常企业从事的一些商业活动，公益公司值得考虑。

### 从 OpenAI 和 Anthropic 学到的招聘经验

Amodei 表示，作为早期员工和创始人，这可能并不令人惊讶，但许多创始人和早期员工都谈论文化以及在公司成长时文化的重要性，尤其是快速扩展的公司。她想强调，当你是小型且快速扩展的公司时，最有价值的货币就是该公司的文化。对她来说，文化实际上是公司价值观的表达。她认为，越清楚地——确定文化和价值观是什么可能感觉很难、很模糊，他们经历这个过程，写使命宣言和价值观，进行文化面试，它永远无法完全捕捉当你走进门成为员工时的感觉，比如「我知道在这里工作是什么感觉」。但她认为，早期真正投入这个过程并说「好吧，它永远无法真正量化。但如果我必须量化，我会如何描述我们的文化？支撑我们作为组织身份的核心价值观是什么？我们如何确保来到公司的人认同这些？」——这些努力真的很重要。她在 Anthropic 学到的最大的教训可能是，你应该尽可能开放和沟通你的文化是什么。因为对那套价值观和愿景感到兴奋的人会被你吸引。他们会说，哇，我们喜欢你们是公益公司，你们在平衡这两件事，你们关心这套主题。对于那不是他们菜的人，越早了解越好。你希望他们读你的网站然后说，嗯，听起来不错，但那不是我真正感兴趣的。所以她认为这是想要吸引正确的人的微妙平衡，在早期员工中几乎极端地强调确保文化契合。

### 如何清晰谈论价值观

Amodei 说，第一，如果你的价值观没有让人有点——如果它们没有制造张力，它们可能太 bland 了。就像，善良、直接。每个人都想要那样。所以如果没有刻意的张力，可能不够具体。第二，你需要能够用 10 个例子支持任何价值观，就像脱口而出。他们有一个价值观是「做有效的简单事」。这来自他们的研究团队，说他们实际上不是来这里搞一堆花哨算法没理由的。他们真的在努力让模型对人们尽可能有用。没有额外的分数给复杂或写这些花哨文档或过度思考。但她认为这个价值观不仅适用于研究。她可以脱口而出解释他们如何在研究之外，在产品、工程、办公室选择、如何设置 off-site 中使用它。她认为，任何时候一个价值观是你很容易就能指出例子的，它更可能 sticky，因为人们会遇到它并感受到那个价值观。

### 让第 200 个员工也能运用价值观

Amodei 说，第一，她认为写下来并在公司内的公开论坛上谈论真的很有价值。在他们的 all hands 中，他们尝试每次 all hands 都提到一个价值观。他们并不总是做得完美，你也不想生搬硬套。但她认为真正强调并让语言超级舒适很重要。你必须确保它真正与人们产生共鸣，否则感觉像痛苦的宣传。但如果它确实产生共鸣，实际上是他们自愿说的。她会听到公司里的其他员工说，嗯，我们考虑在 off-site 举办这个活动，但我们决定在办公室举办，因为我们想做有效的简单事。当她听到这种或不同价值观的版本时，她总是会眼前一亮，因为这意味着这对人们来说是实际的，适用于他们。另一件事是，如果价值观对你不起作用，不要害怕退休它们。有时作为创始人你觉得某件事真的很对，但它就是不够 sticky。也许只是你说错了。不一定是底层的核心部分是错的。但她认为，感到舒适地迭代，直到有人说，实际上，那样更能引起我的共鸣。人们应该 meme-ify 自己。另一件她认为有趣的事是，他们的一些价值观不是来自创始人，而是来自公司。有人开始使用一个术语，然后他们说，那实际上是公司的价值观，太好了。她认为这有助于员工感觉他们对价值观也有所有权。这不是强加给他们的东西。

### 非显而易见的招聘特质

Amodei 认为，在 Anthropic 最重要的品质之一，尤其是，是愿意尊重地谈论广泛话题。他们从事非常前沿的技术，很多与之相关的事情可能感觉复杂或有争议。AI 有巨大的潜力帮助人们。如果不够小心，它也有巨大的潜力被滥用。所以他们必须解决这些挑战。他们把这视为核心工作。所以如果人们不愿意有尊重的分歧并且非常开放，比如即使说我们对这个话题有完全不同的看法，但我必须能够与你在这些话题上尊重地交流——这实际上是一项非常特殊的技能，尤其是当它可能是关于感觉如此根本的事情时。这是他们寻找的。他们也在文化面试中评估。他们问人们，你持有的一些不寻常的信念是什么？你在不舒服的情况下如何捍卫这些信念，因为你觉得这是正确的事？他们不是在寻找特定的信念体系。他们寻找的是能够说，嘿，我觉得这是正确的事，也许不受欢迎，但我真的坚持了，或者我改变了主意，因为我意识到我在这件事上不对。

### 平衡深思熟虑与简洁（工程招聘）

Amodei 表示，对于工程师和研究，他们总是在一个有趣的舞蹈中：什么时候投资于需要做的基础研究和工程工作，什么时候只是发布和迭代某样东西？她认为这与很多公司类似，但在像 Anthropic 这样的地方特别具有挑战性，因为有时投资于不一定看起来会对客户有即时回报的基础研究，是创造突破的最佳方式。她希望有完美的公式。这更像是艺术而非科学。但至少在研究方面，他们真的尝试多样化花在优化模型中特定品质或工具或系统上的时间，以及团队一定量的开放探索研究时间。在工程方面类似。他们想给人们一些范围，让他们能够真正重新思考和重新想象他们正在构建的产品以及为什么。她认为这与研究发展如此之快高度相关，就像编码例子中，你并不总是知道接下来会发生什么，事情可以瞬间改变。你可以突然说，嗯，有一个令人难以置信的新用例，我们实际上应该围绕它构建产品。所以在某种程度上，他们总是想给人们空间去重新想象他们如何甚至为客户提供价值。他们还必须设定产品路线图，朝着该路线图构建，并确保他们正在做所有非常重要但有时不性感的工程工作。她认为它落在 60-40 或 40-60 或 50-50 之间的某个地方，有段时间他们真的在冲刺他们长期工作的特定发布。但她认为一点开放式的探索心态对他们来说是重要的品质。

### 团队轮换与探索时间

Amodei 说他们 heavily 依赖团队轮换。她发现，实际上让一群人处于探索模式更容易进入那种 flow。如果你试图分配自己的时间 80% 对 20%，有时有效，她认为对某些员工确实如此。但在大多数情况下，专门有一个探索团队——你在做探索性努力。这是一个构建产品或研究工作流的团队，相当明确，他们在执行。不一定不那么令人兴奋，因为有很多工作要做。但通常人们喜欢在这两种不同的工作状态和 flow 之间切换。她认为这也是员工学习的更有趣的方式，因为他们既能看到探索的价值，也能看到探索后来对客户的影响。

### 关于 AI 未来的常见误解

Amodei 认为，不同用例的推出和实施，甚至在不同行业之间，将非常不均衡。在世界的某些角落，很难想象某些公司今天不利用 AI。但世界上有大量人从未使用过 AI，从未想过如何使用 AI，可能很多年都不会。所以她认为思考这将如何渗透到经济中真的很有趣，无论是在消费者层面，还是在应用层、基础架构层、企业、中型企业。她认为没有人能真正预测技术将如何被采用，或在哪里、如何、何时。她认为这将是一个真正有趣也许有点混乱的时期，随着世界不同地区开始或多或少地利用 AI。

### 与兄弟姐妹共同创业的惊喜

Amodei 表示，她从 Stripe 获得了很大灵感，因为近距离看到 John 和 Patrick 兄弟共同创办公司。她很幸运与 Dario 拥有极其互补的技能组合。实际上一直非常有趣。他们一直想一起做点什么。他们在 OpenAI 共事过。但自童年起就梦想能一起构建某样东西。对她来说，每天与他共事是一种巨大的荣幸。他们从左右脑不同角度共同解决问题的能力非常独特。

### 2035 年的 AI

Amodei 认为 2035 年世界会看起来很不同。她的预期是会有很多地方很难想象没有人工智能这个角落如何运作。就像今天，很难想象 10 年或 20 年前我们视为理所当然的许多基础技术。如果想象互联网和搜索，20 年前甚至 25 年前还不常见。无法想象没有谷歌地图人们如何到达某地？你怎么知道去哪？她认为人工智能会有很多这样的时刻，你会说，嗯，我怎么可能自己上网购物？显然，AI 助手会帮我去找想要的东西，不管是什么。她不知道具体会是什么样子。但她认为也会有很多看起来 surprisingly 相同的东西，就像想象 20 或 25 年前。是的，我们更容易找到信息和到达某地，但人类交往和互动的基本方式她认为变化不大。所以她的预期会看到另一轮。可能比第一轮互联网浪潮的周期稍快一点。

### 公益公司是正确的选择

Amodei 反思，回顾创办 Anthropic 以来，当时感觉是大事但事后看来只是 ho-hum 的，比如选哪个办公室。他们成长得比预期快得多。他们试图想在哪里设立总部，选了一个很好的办公室，但大约一年半就坐不下了。她认为，可能——她不想说 ho-hum——他们花了很多时间思考注册问题。应该注册为 LLC、C 公司、非营利还是 PBC？事后看来，PBC 对他们来说是如此明显的正确决定。但他们花了一两个月讨论才正式决定注册为什么。现在回头看，感觉那根本不应该是个难题，但当时他们没有远见知道组织的形态会是什么样子。

## Full Content

### Anthropic as an AI Safety and Research Company

Daniela Amodei explained that Anthropic focuses on how to develop tools and systems in a way that is robustly safe across the stack. They use a technique called Constitutional AI that helps imbue Claude with a sense of ethics. A number of different documents went into helping train Claude with Constitutional AI, including the UN Declaration of Human Rights. For releasing new models or products, they have a framework called the Responsible Scaling Policy (RSP). The RSP is essentially a set of guidelines indicating how and when they will release models to ensure they're safe. They use different AI safety levels—five of them, modeled along biological safety levels. They look for defining a concrete set of safety and security standards.

### Balancing Research and Commercialization

Amodei said that one of the most interesting and unusual things about running a company like Anthropic is that they're almost part research lab, part tech startup, with a huge emphasis on public benefit mission. Thinking about how to balance all three in service of the mission and providing value for customers is one of the great joys of working at a place like Anthropic. For the research-to-product balance, they found that collaboration between product and research helps make both teams much stronger. As new research capabilities are developed, it opens up new avenues in product that wouldn't have existed before. A recent example: Claude has been particularly useful for developers in writing code. Many products developed over the past two to three months have been geared around making that process easier for software developers. If they hadn't known the models would be really good at coding, they wouldn't have necessarily designed parts of the product that way. There's an iterative process: as more customers use them for coding, they help train the models to make them stronger in coding and provide more product support in that area.

### Public Benefit Corporation (PBC)

Amodei explained that when starting Anthropic, they spent a reasonable amount of time thinking about what structure to incorporate as. They had the benefit of having seen different models up close. When incorporating as a PBC, they were thinking about a very practical way to balance commercial interests they foresaw with the public benefit intentions behind founding a company so invested in safety. They ultimately decided that public benefit corporations are a really nice blend of those two areas. It's a C corporation, structured very similarly to a Delaware C corporation in most ways. But the one exception is they have the ability to balance commercial needs against public benefit interests. This felt like a really pragmatic approach. She would say depending on the goal of the startup being founded, PBCs are worth looking at if there's a component of the business that aims to balance the public interest with commercial activities.

### Hiring Lessons from OpenAI and Anthropic

Amodei said that as an early employee and founder, it's probably not surprising, but so many founders and early employees talk about culture and how important it is when growing a company, particularly one scaling really rapidly. She wants to echo that the most valuable currency when you're a small and quickly scaling company is the culture of that company. To her, culture is really an expression of the company's values. The more clearly—it can feel really hard and fuzzy to pin down what your culture and values are. They go through the process, write the mission statement and values, have culture interviews, and it never completely captures the feeling when you walk in the doors as an employee. But she thinks the effort to really invest in that process early and say, okay, it can never really be quantified, but if I had to quantify it, how would I describe our culture? What are the core values that underpin who we are as an organization and how do we make sure people that come to the company are on board with that—that's really important. Probably the biggest lesson she's learned at Anthropic is that you should really be as open and communicative about what your culture is as possible. The people excited about that set of values and vision will be attracted to you. For people who that's not their cup of tea, it's much better to learn that early. You want them to read your website and say, well, that sounds nice, that's not really what I'm interested in. So it's this delicate balance of wanting to attract the right kind of people and having this almost extreme emphasis on ensuring cultural alignment in early employees.

### How to Talk About Values Clearly

Amodei said, number one, if your values are not creating tension, they're probably too bland. "Be kind, be direct"—everyone wants that. So if there's not an intentional tension, they're probably not specific enough. Number two, you need to be able to back up any value with 10 examples off the top of your head. A value they have is "do the simple thing that works." This comes from their research team saying they're not here to have fancy algorithms for no reason—they're really trying to make the models as useful as possible. No extra points for being complicated. But she thinks that value applies outside of just research. She could explain how they use it in product, engineering, office selection, how they set up off-sites. Anytime a value is one where you can point to examples really easily, it's more likely to be sticky because people will run into it and feel that value.

### Getting the 200th Employee to Apply Values

Amodei said that writing them down and talking about them in public forums within the company is really valuable. In their all-hands, they try to mention a value every all-hands. They don't always do it perfectly. But really emphasizing and making the language super comfortable is important. You have to make sure it actually resonates with people; otherwise it feels like painful propaganda. But if it does resonate, it's something they volunteer themselves. She hears other employees say, "we were considering hosting this event off-site, but we decided to host it at the office because we wanted to do the simple thing that works." She always lights up when she hears that. Don't be afraid to retire values if they're not working. Sometimes something feels really right as the founder but it's just not sticky. It's maybe just saying it the wrong way. People should be meme-ifying themselves. Some of their values didn't come from the founders—they came from the company. Someone started using a term and they said, that's actually a value of the company. It helps employees feel like they have ownership over the values too.

### Non-Obvious Trait When Hiring

Amodei thinks one of the most important qualities at Anthropic is a willingness to talk about a wide range of topics respectfully. They work on cutting-edge frontier technology. A lot of things that bump into it can feel complicated or controversial. AI has incredible potential to help people. It also has incredible potential to be misused if they're not careful. They have to address those challenges. They view that as their core job. So if people are not willing to have respectful disagreements and be very open-minded—even to say we're coming from completely different opinions about this topic, but I have to be able to engage with you respectfully—it's a very particular skill. They look for this. They assess for it in their culture interview. They ask people: what are some unusual beliefs that you hold? How have you defended those beliefs in uncomfortable situations because you felt it was the right thing to do? They're not looking for a particular belief system. They're looking for the ability to say, I felt this was the correct thing and maybe it wasn't popular, but I really stood by this, or I changed my mind because I actually realized I wasn't right about this.

### Balancing Thoughtfulness and Simplicity (Engineering Hires)

Amodei said that for both engineers and research, they're always in an interesting dance of when to invest in fundamental research and engineering work versus when to just launch and iterate. At a place like Anthropic, it's particularly challenging because sometimes investing in fundamental research that doesn't necessarily look like it's going to have immediate returns for customers is the best way to create breakthroughs. She wishes she had a perfect formula. It's much more of an art than a science. On the research side, they really try to diversify the amount of time spent optimizing particular qualities versus some amount of open exploratory research time. On the engineering side, similar—they want to give people scope to really rethink and reimagine what products they're building and why. Because research is developing so quickly, you don't always know what's coming. Things can change on a dime. They always want to give people the space to reimagine how they're providing value to customers. They also have to set a product roadmap and build towards it. It falls somewhere in the middle of 60-40 or 50-50. A little bit of that open-ended exploratory mindset is an important quality.

### Team Rotations and Exploration Time

Amodei said they lean pretty heavily on team rotations. It's actually easier to have a group of people that's just in exploratory mode because it's easier to get into that flow. If you're trying to split your own time 80% vs 20%, sometimes that works. But in the majority of cases, having a purposely exploratory team works better. It's also a more interesting way for employees to learn—they get to see the value of exploration and the impact that exploration can later have on customers.

### Common Misconception About AI Future

Amodei thinks the degree to which the rollout and implementation of different use cases, even across different sectors, is going to be very uneven. In some pockets of the world, it's impossible to imagine certain companies not leveraging AI today. But there's a whole swath of the world that has never used AI, has never thought about how to use AI, and might not for many more years. Thinking about how this will permeate through the economy—at the consumer level, application layer, infrastructure layer, enterprises, mid-market businesses—is really interesting. She doesn't think anyone can really predict the ways the technology will be adopted, or the places, or how, or when. It's going to be a really interesting and maybe a little bit chaotic period.

### Co-Founding with a Sibling

Amodei had great inspiration from Stripe because she saw John and Patrick co-found a company together up close. She's really fortunate that Dario and she have extremely complimentary skill sets. It's actually just been really fun. They've always wanted to work on something together. For her, it's actually a huge privilege to get to work with him every day. Their ability to tackle problems together from the left brain, right brain perspective is very unique.

### AI in 2035

Amodei thinks in 2035 the world is going to look really different. Her expectation is there will be a lot of places where it will be so hard to imagine how this pocket worked without artificial intelligence. Just like today it's really hard to imagine so many baseline technologies from 10 or 20 years ago. There will be a lot of moments like that in AI. But she also thinks there will be a lot of things that look surprisingly the same—fundamental things about how humans engage and interact together that don't change very much. She expects to see another round of that, maybe on a slightly faster cycle than the first round of the Internet wave.

### PBC Was the Right Move

Amodei reflected that they thought a lot about the incorporation question—should they be an LLC, C corporation, nonprofit, or PBC? In retrospect, it was so obvious that PBC was the right decision for them. But it took them a month or two of discussion before they formally decided. Now looking back, it doesn't feel like that should have been a difficult problem at all.
